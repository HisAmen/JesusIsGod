    Throughout this documentation you will notice how the names for God in Hebrew, especially in the Old Testament, are always inconsistent. The writer can speak of the same person but use a completely different word to refer to him. For example, sometimes the writer might use the word “Adonai” but then he would call the exact same person to whom he is referring to “Yehova”. However, all of this is explained in later explanations.

    Before we go into the explanations and verses, Hebrews had used various different names for God, some words like YHWH were not even pronounced by the prophets in the Old Testament consistently, because of how Holy his name was. Instead the names like Adonai were used in its places. However, these variations don’t imply a different being but reflects the richness and layered meaning of God’s names in Hebrew.

    📝 Research Notes.

    1. 1In Jewish tradition, Adonai (אֲדֹנָי), meaning "Lord," is used instead of the divine name YHWH (יהוה) out of reverence and to avoid the potential of mispronouncing or taking the name in vain.

    2. YHWH (יהוה), the sacred divine name, is almost always translated into Greek as κύριος (Kyrios), meaning “Lord” – often without the definite article, suggesting a proper-name usage and continuity with Old Testament reverential reading. Adonai (אֲדֹנָי), meaning “Lord” as a respectful title, is likewise translated as κύριος ((Kyrios) (context is everything)), according to the work of theologian Larry Hurtado and others.

    3. The word kyrios originally meant “power” or “might,” but over time it came to mean “lord” or “master.” When the Old Testament was translated from Hebrew to Greek, it used kyrios for “Lord” wherever the words YHWH or Adonai occurred in the Hebrew text.
      Example: In the septuagint manuscripts (LXX) virtually every time YHWH or Adonai are translated as Kyrios.

    4. In the Septuagint (LXX), YHWH (God’s personal name) is translated as Kyrios, so for Greek readers, Kyrios already carried divine weight.

    5. Moving forward, Kyrios and Theos are used together in the New Testament (e.g., John 1:1 and John 1:14). While Kyrios is not directly used in John 1:1, it refers to the “Word” as being the Lord Jesus. John 1:14 states that the Word became flesh and dwelt among us, meaning Jesus is called Theos and Kyrios, dismantling any ideas that Jesus is not referenced as being God or part of the Trinity.

    6. John 20:28 — Thomas explicitly calls Jesus both Lord (Kyrios) and God (Theos), In the same sentence. Jesus also receives Thomas praise. That is a clear divine claim.

    7. Romans 9:5 uses Theos in reference to Christ as "God blessed forever," and Romans 10:9 states it was "God (Theos)" who raised Jesus from the dead, affirming Paul’s belief in Jesus as God, by looking into John’s work (John 1:1 - John 1:14).

    8. Early Church Fathers believed in Jesus as being divine.

    Common Disagreement and Fault Exposed.

    Claim: Jehovah’s Witnesses argue that Jesus is the “firstborn of creation,” meaning He is a created being and not God.

    Biblical reference often cited:
    Colossians 1:15 – “He is the image of the invisible God, the firstborn of every creature.”

    Explanation Including Usage of Greek:
    The Greek word πρωτότοκος (prōtotokos) means “firstborn,” primarily conveying rank, status, or preeminence rather than “first created.” In Jewish culture, the firstborn son held a special position of authority and inheritance rights(e.g., Exodus 4:22, Psalm 89:27). When the New Testament describes Jesus as πρωτότοκος πάσης κτίσεως (“firstborn of all creation,” Colossians 1:15), it speaks of His preeminent status over creation, not that He was the first created being.

    Critical:If the New Testament intended to express that Jesus was created, it would likely use a different form such as πρωτόκτιστος (prōtoktistos)—a rare but theoretically possible meaning “first created.” However, this term does not appear in the Scriptures. Instead, words related to κτίζω (ktizō), meaning “to create,” or nouns like κτίσμα (ktisma) (“a created thing”) would be used to denote creation.

    Also, an early church Father, Clement of Alexandria, pointed out the following in his commentary:

    “He [Christ] is called ‘firstborn’ not because He is the first thing created, but because He is the preeminent One, the heir of all things; for to be ‘firstborn’ is a title of dignity and authority, not of origin or creation.” - Clement of Alexandria, The Stromata (Miscellanies), Book 5, Chapter 14.
